OKRs: Student Notes

Objectives & Key Results Training

Complete guide to understanding and implementing OKRs in your business. Use these notes alongside the presentation and workshop.

What Are OKRs?

An OKR is a goal-setting framework with two parts:

  1. Objective: The qualitative goal — what you want to achieve
  2. Key Results: How you measure success — specific, quantitative outcomes

OKRs help you decide what matters most and track progress toward it. They're used by Google, Amazon, Intel, and 90%+ of venture-backed companies.

Key Principle:

OKRs bridge your long-term vision (where you're going) with short-term direction (what you do this quarter).

Bold vision WITHOUT fluff. Specific direction WITHOUT losing the dream.

Not OKRs

These are commonly confused with OKRs but are not:

  • Tasks: "Fix the bug" is a task, not an OKR
  • Strategy: "Improve marketing" is too vague
  • Metrics: "Track page views" is tracking, not a goal
  • To-do lists: A list of everything you want to do

Why OKRs Matter

Most entrepreneurs and leaders have a clarity problem:

  • Too many priorities → Everything is important, so nothing is
  • Misalignment → Teams work on different goals
  • Vanity metrics → Tracking things that don't matter
  • Busyness → A sign you've lost focus

The Core Insight:

"If you're afraid to fail, you're not going to make radical innovation."

OKRs make failure safe. You should miss some OKRs every quarter. That means you're stretching.

How OKRs Solve This

Problem OKR Solution
Too many priorities Focus: Limit to 3-5 OKRs per quarter
Misalignment Alignment: Everyone's OKRs connect vertically and horizontally
Vanity metrics Real metrics: Key Results track what moves the business
No strategy Strategy first: OKRs start with "What's our vision?"

The F.A.C.T.S. Framework

John Doerr (venture capitalist, author of "Measure What Matters") created this framework. It's the backbone of healthy OKRs.

F - FOCUS

Decide on 3-5 priorities per quarter. Don't do everything.

Most entrepreneurs see 100 opportunities. If you pursue all of them, you complete none.

⚠️ Reality check: If you have more than 5 company-level OKRs, you don't have priorities. You have a to-do list.

A - ALIGNMENT

OKRs ladder up from company → team → individual.

Company OKR: "Become #1 AI-first platform for entrepreneurs"
  ↓
Product Team OKR: "Build 5 new AI features"
  ↓
Product Manager OKR: "Launch Feature A by mid-quarter"

When alignment works, decisions are easy: "Does this support our OKR?"

C - COMMITMENT

You set OKRs together. Then you commit to them. Then you don't change them mid-quarter.

OKRs only work if there's commitment. Every shiny object derails you without it.

What changes mid-quarter: Tactics, resources, timeline

What doesn't change: The goal itself, the deadline

T - TRACKING

Review progress weekly. Adjust tactics as needed.

Most teams set OKRs, then lose visibility. By August, nobody remembers what they committed to.

Weekly check-in: 15 minutes. "Are we on track? What's blocking us? What do we need to adjust?"

S - STRETCHING

Set OKRs where you're 70% confident of achievement.

  • Too easy (95% confidence) → no growth
  • Too hard (50% confidence) → team panics
  • 70% confidence → team grows + still wins

The 70% Rule:

Google targets 70% OKR achievement. If you hit 100% of OKRs every quarter, your OKRs are too easy.

70% achievement = Winning.

Objective vs. Key Result

Objective

  • Qualitative (the "what")
  • Inspirational ("Become the go-to platform for solopreneurs")
  • Action-oriented (something you DO)
  • 1-2 sentences max

Key Result

  • Quantitative (the "how much")
  • Specific metric ("Grow users from 50K to 150K")
  • Time-bound ("By end of Q2")
  • Measurable (no debate about success/failure)

Example

Objective: "Establish ourselves as the #1 trusted AI partner for enterprise"

Key Results:

  • KR1: Land 5 Fortune 500 customers
  • KR2: Grow annual contract value from $50K to $100K
  • KR3: Achieve 85% customer retention
  • KR4: Build NPS from 32 to 55

Two Types of OKRs

Committed OKRs

  • Confidence: 90%+
  • Purpose: Keep the lights on. Maintain business continuity.
  • Example: "Hit $10M ARR"
  • If you miss: Requires explanation. This is a failure.

Aspirational OKRs

  • Confidence: 60-70%
  • Purpose: Moonshots. Breakthrough thinking.
  • Example: "Launch a new product line"
  • If you miss: Learning opportunity. This is expected.

The Right Mix

60% committed + 40% aspirational

  • All committed → Team gets bored, no growth
  • All aspirational → Team burns out, can't achieve basics
  • Balanced → Team grows + wins + stays motivated

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Setting Too Many OKRs

20 OKRs = nobody can focus. Result: nothing gets done. Stick to 3-5.

❌ Confusing OKRs with Tasks

❌ "Fix the bug" | ✅ "Improve product stability (reduce crash rate from 2% to 0.5%)"

❌ Making OKRs Too Easy

If you hit 100% of OKRs consistently, they're not ambitious enough.

❌ Ignoring Alignment

Team OKRs that don't support company OKRs = teams working at cross-purposes.

❌ Set-and-Forget

OKRs are only valuable if you track them weekly. Skip this, and OKRs become shelf-ware.

❌ Using Vanity Metrics

❌ "Increase page views" | ✅ "Convert 5% of visitors to paying customers"

❌ Not Involving Your Team

You set OKRs alone → low buy-in → low execution. Collaborate instead.

How to Track OKRs

Weekly Check-In (15 minutes)

Template:

OKR Target Current Progress Status
KR1: Users 50K→100K 100K 75K 75% 🟢 On track
KR2: Retention 40%→60% 60% 48% 80% 🟡 At risk

Quarterly Review (60 minutes)

  1. Score each OKR (0-100%)
  2. Determine reasons (hit or miss)
  3. Extract learnings (what worked, what didn't)
  4. Plan next quarter (what's next)

70% is GOOD

If you hit 75% of OKRs, that's success. It means you stretched and learned.

Real Examples

Example: SaaS Startup

Objective: "Become the customer's first choice"

  • KR1: Generate 2,000 qualified leads through content (from 200)
  • KR2: Achieve 40% user retention by end of Q2
  • KR3: Land 3 Fortune 500 customers
  • KR4: Improve product NPS from 32 to 55

Example: Solopreneur Coach

Objective: "Build a 7-figure coaching business that runs on systems, not me"

  • KR1: Generate $500K revenue from group programs
  • KR2: Build email list to 50K engaged subscribers
  • KR3: Develop and launch 1 certification program

Final Thoughts

OKRs are simple in concept but powerful in practice. Setting good OKRs is a skill you'll improve with practice.

Your first OKR won't be perfect. That's fine. Set it anyway. Track it. Learn from it. Improve next quarter.

Founders who've mastered OKRs consistently move faster, make better decisions, scale their businesses, and have happier teams.

Now go set your OKRs. 💜
